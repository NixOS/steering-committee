  - Context: a question came up about what the criteria should be for being listed on the commercial support page on nixos.org, prompted by a recent PR and the lack of a clear process for resolving such questions. The SC discussed both the specific situation (kept off the record) and the general policy gap (below).

      - @tomberek: raised the underlying situation as an example of an in-between case where it's unclear what next steps are or who's responsible.
      - @cafkafk: there exists a spectrum — some people we obviously want associated with nixos and some we obviously don't. *Where* that line is, is a valid question we should be able to answer as a community, and right now we don't really have a way to.
      - @Ericson2314: a useful angle — has the listed party set up anything resembling a business (registered entity, trademark, domain name, etc.)? Just a github profile makes the commercial support page look less professional. A higher effort bar (similar to what early one-person commercial Nix offerings did) seems reasonable.
      - @cafkafk: that's a good baseline checkpoint to keep the page from being a free-for-all. But there's also a harder question about brand association — analogous to how we think about who we'd accept sponsorship from (e.g. the community would not want certain defense-industry sponsorship even if individual SC members differ). What we accept *advertising* on our site is related to what we accept sponsorship from.
      - @philiptaron: a key concern is process — when a single thumbs-down with no further engagement effectively kills a PR with no clear rationale, that's bad standard operating procedure, regardless of whether the underlying call is right. The SC should provide clarity that we support the moderation team making moderation decisions of this kind, *and* that there can be a higher bar than just a github profile, *and* that a single person's reaction should not be the totality of the matter.
      - @cafkafk: agreed — and this isn't just about any one PR. The pattern of "someone reacts negatively and the PR dies" is something we see across nixpkgs too (e.g. style-change PRs). The systemic problem is the lack of a clear direction and criteria for how these decisions should be made. Solving one instance without addressing the system doesn't fix it.
      - @tomberek: letting these situations fester perpetuates a grievance cycle. There's also a real risk of "parallel construction" — pivoting between framings (moderation vs categorization) makes outcomes look arbitrary. Two angles forward: ask marketing to decide strictly on categorization grounds, or engage moderation to clarify whether a given input is individual or team-level. Also: generally we've biased toward individuals over corporations, and we should be careful not to flip that without thinking it through.
      - @cafkafk: structural caveat — starting a business is very cheap in some places (e.g. Denmark) and a real barrier in others, so a "registered business" bar is uneven globally. Also, decisions about who appears on the commercial support page are effectively a kind of *reverse sponsorship* call, which feels close to SC's existing remit (we already make calls on sponsors). It's not really a moderation problem (no two-individual conflict) and marketing alone shouldn't have to make the final call on de-risking community-facing partnerships.
      - @tomberek: marketing should be free to proceed in most cases and only escalate when unclear; SC shouldn't be a blocker on every single listing.
      - @cafkafk: agreed — proposals could be floated to SC with e.g. a 7-day react window, default-accept if no response, so SC is never blocking but never uninformed. Ideally on zulip.
      - @cafkafk: an important fairness point — if a criterion is "be a business", that has to be *the* bar, not "be a business + pass an unspecified vibe check". Otherwise the next applicant who jumps through the stated hoop and is then rejected on unstated grounds has a legitimate grievance, and the goalposts-moved frustration is validated.
      - @tomberek: agreed — bar + secret requirement is exactly the pattern that creates blow-ups.
      - @cafkafk: if we do reject a specific application on the "commercial entity" criterion, we should also be upfront with the applicant that there can be additional brand-related considerations that we haven't fully formalized yet, and that we'll communicate those criteria as soon as we have them. Better than leaving applicants in the dark.
      - @roberth: "a business" is too vague — suggest business registration of any kind, including sole proprietor.
      - @cafkafk: VAT number is usually a good concrete bar — public, broadly international, and a clear signal of having a business entity of some kind.
      - @philiptaron: initially proposed an immediate SC vote on requiring commercial entities, then retracted — more input needed from the board and marketing team before voting on something that would become public policy.
      - @Ericson2314: agreed — would want board and marketing team's explicit input before voting on public rules. SC has a good role here given the cross-cutting, multi-team nature of the issue; this could be a positive resolution for everyone.

    - Main question for SC: where do you want to go directionally? And how should this be achieved?
      With these answers a lot of decision making goes a lot faster.
      It worked out this time because the question was binary this time (a given cost and benefit)
- @tomberek: are there specific dimensions of vision along which more clarity would help? Arbitrary big picture vision is too open-ended.
- @fricklerhandwerk: same thing I've been telling everyone for 3 years — do you want more structure? Directionally more company-like (in parentheses: not at all like a company, because it's a community thing) with the attached risks and benefits. That's the main dimension.
- @cafkafk: hard balance. We're all volunteers, so a fast way to make volunteers hate you is to tell them to do a specific thing instead of the thing they're actually passionate about (which will also change in 2 weeks). It's also hard to mitigate the risk of mostly-users (not developers) steering the roadmap for the people who actually have to do the work. So an ideal solution can't be exactly like a company: a company has to satisfy capitalism / make money, while we have to keep contributors happy enough that they keep contributing. Our contributors are effectively our customers. We need to make all these groups happy at the same time: users feel heard, developers don't feel told what to do but are doing things they want to do.
- @fricklerhandwerk: resource calculation is multi-dimensional, not just money. Has ideas — remembers how the second board formed; there were interviews with other foundations and the sentiment was: gather resources to help people do what they would already do, but better. Crucially *enable* volunteers, not tell them what to do. Sometimes that's literally providing infrastructure (no volunteer will do that). Striking that balance is the politics part.
- @cafkafk: important to involve e.g. nixpkgs people because they often have thematic stretches of time for specific things (cited Blender's bug-squash periods). That works because developers think it's fun. If @cafkafk walked into the nixpkgs chat and announced "this is the summer of fixing rust boxes" they'd push back. Lean into the existing organic patterns (e.g. ZHF) to get real buy-in, and use that to derive a roadmap, rather than imposing one.
- @fricklerhandwerk: that's already implementation level. Everyone has very specific implementation ideas. We haven't yet achieved enough coherence — there's something that could be distilled that the SC and foundation could unanimously buy into.
- @roberth: yes structural approach is beneficial — there's work that doesn't get done or doesn't get done on time (chiefly security and maintenance).
- @philiptaron: one thing I love about nix/nixos is that it appears that money can't do anything. People try to give us money/credits/goodies and reliably nothing happens unless a specific person takes the work on (like Wolfgang with GitHub Actions, or @fricklerhandwerk with documentation). I haven't seen things in nix move forward without a specific person who has dedicated their own sense of what good looks like. I see this as a sign of health, not ill health. I'd be loath to move to a top-down world — we'd lose much of what I love about nix.
- @fricklerhandwerk: would be careful — nixpkgs is very different from nix itself and the surrounding ecosystem. Reference: "Working in Public" taxonomy — we're not a federation or a stadium, more like a multitude of kingdoms with nixpkgs as the anarchist state in the middle. Agrees with the "no bullshit" attitude but it's more complicated than that.
- @cafkafk: this clarifies that there are perhaps two separate things: (1) money we have to receive and have a plan for — much easier to have a roadmap there because people are being paid and it's reasonable to direct paid work; (2) roadmap for unstructured volunteer work — much harder. So it's much more palatable to say "here's the roadmap and mechanism for funded initiatives" than "here's the 5-year plan for nixpkgs that everyone must follow".
- @tomberek: nixpkgs is indeed very different and may require differently shaped solutions. Question: what levers do we have, and do we want to use them? Even a vision document needs someone with time — either someone leading and doing sweat equity (@Ericson2314 as canonical example: vision + actual time to make it happen) or someone driving via proxy by organizing/orchestrating (e.g. @fricklerhandwerk). Even with funding it still comes down to an individual to shepherd it. Proposal: focus on individual project leads with scoped efforts that can be funded. Multiple people can explore different visions and coexist — much more aligned with our values. Don't put all our eggs in one basket; small enough efforts that they can be re-upped if they bear fruit.
- @fricklerhandwerk: idea of the vision is to step back and ask: in this multi-dimensional configuration space, where do we want to be in 5–10 years, then how do we achieve it, what resources/assets/procedures do we have? Reports from the weekend conversation with Silvan and Sebastian: when you start accounting for things, things are surprisingly expensive — nixpkgs is hundreds of millions of dollars of volunteer work. Marginal value of money is not the same for everyone, and people have different value metrics for non-monetary things too. Sebastian and Silvan agree that finding consensus and alignment needs substantial time that neither the SC nor the board has. Silvan proposed @fricklerhandwerk talk to everyone and distill something agreeable, meet at NixCon (this year or next) to lock in the remaining details, and come out with a public statement.
